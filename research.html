Research

My research sits at the intersection of urban economics, public finance, and applied microeconomics. A common thread runs through all of it: understanding how economic systems produce unequal outcomes — and what that means for policy.

My dissertation focused on property taxes and urban transportation, with particular attention to the distributional, spatial, and racial dimensions of both. Before graduate school, I spent two years at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York working on consumer credit, labor markets, and housing — much of that research found its way into peer-reviewed journals and Fed policy discussions.

I left academia not because I stopped caring about these questions, but because I wanted to work closer to where decisions actually get made. That instinct is what's driving me toward AI now — the same questions about markets, fairness, and institutions are playing out in real time, at scale, and the answers matter.

Academic Publications

"The Problem Has Existed Over Endless Years: Racialized Difference in Commuting, 1980–2018"

devin michelle bunten, Ellen Fu, Lyndsey Rolheiser, and Chris Severen

Journal of Urban Economics, 2024  ·  DOI  ·  SSRN

TLDR: Racial gaps in commuting time have persisted — and in some cases widened — over nearly four decades, even after accounting for income, geography, and occupation. This paper documents those gaps and explores the structural factors that sustain them.

Working Papers

"The Financial Burdens of Property Taxes: Evidence from Philadelphia"

Ellen Fu

Working Paper, SSRN 2022  ·  SSRN

TLDR: Property taxes are often assumed to be proportional or even progressive. This paper uses administrative data from Philadelphia to show that effective tax rates are substantially higher for lower-value properties, with significant implications for wealth accumulation in low-income and minority neighborhoods.
