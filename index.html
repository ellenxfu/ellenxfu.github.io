Ellen Fu, PhD

Economist. Strategist. Believer that markets should work better for everyone.

👋 Hi, I'm Ellen. I'm an applied economist based in San Francisco with a decade of experience using data, causal inference, and economic reasoning to understand how markets and institutions shape people's lives.

My research roots are in urban economics and public policy — studying how property taxes burden low-income homeowners, how racial gaps in commuting have persisted across decades, and how labor markets leave people behind. That work wasn't just academic for me. It reflected a genuine belief that careful, rigorous analysis is a precondition for meaningful change.

After finishing my PhD at Wharton, I joined Cornerstone Research, where I lead teams on complex antitrust, competition, and merger matters for Fortune 500 clients. The work has sharpened my ability to structure hard problems quickly, communicate across technical and non-technical audiences, and manage high-stakes projects under pressure.

Alongside my consulting work, I've been increasingly engaged with the economics of AI — how it's reshaping labor markets, housing, consumer finance, and the regulatory landscape. These are the same questions I've always cared about, playing out now at a new scale and speed.

Outside of work, I'm usually training for my next race, eating my way through a new city, or planning a trip somewhere I've never been.

Research

My research sits at the intersection of urban economics, public finance, and applied microeconomics. A common thread runs through all of it: understanding how economic systems produce unequal outcomes — and what that means for policy.

My dissertation focused on property taxes and urban transportation, with particular attention to the distributional, spatial, and racial dimensions of both. Before graduate school, I spent two years at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York working on consumer credit, labor markets, and housing — much of that research found its way into peer-reviewed journals and Fed policy discussions.

I left academia not because I stopped caring about these questions, but because I wanted to work closer to where decisions actually get made. That instinct is what's driving me toward AI now — the same questions about markets, fairness, and institutions are playing out in real time, at scale, and the answers matter.

Academic Publications

"The Problem Has Existed Over Endless Years: Racialized Difference in Commuting, 1980–2018"

devin michelle bunten, Ellen Fu, Lyndsey Rolheiser, and Chris Severen

Journal of Urban Economics, 2024  ·  DOI  ·  SSRN

Racial gaps in commuting time have persisted — and in some cases widened — over nearly four decades, even after accounting for income, geography, and occupation. This paper documents those gaps and explores the structural factors that sustain them.

Working Papers

"The Financial Burdens of Property Taxes: Evidence from Philadelphia"

Ellen Fu

Working Paper, SSRN 2022  ·  SSRN

Property taxes are often assumed to be proportional or even progressive. This paper uses administrative data from Philadelphia to show that effective tax rates are substantially higher for lower-value properties, with significant implications for wealth accumulation in low-income and minority neighborhoods.

Teaching

Teaching has always been one of the parts of academic life I loved most — not lecturing, exactly, but the back-and-forth of helping someone work through a hard idea until it clicks. I've taught and designed courses across a pretty wide range of topics, all connected by a common interest in how economics intersects with cities, policy, and people.

The economics of housing supply and demand, with attention to affordability, zoning, neighborhood change, and the policy levers available to local governments. One of my favorite courses to teach — the material connects directly to debates playing out in every American city.
